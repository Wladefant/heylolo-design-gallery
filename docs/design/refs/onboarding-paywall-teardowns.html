Onboarding + Paywall Teardowns — Top Parent-Paid Kids Apps

Research doc for HeyLolo (elumi-kids) W1–W2 build: onboarding trim (#178), paywall alignment, 14-day no-card trial. Screen-by-screen teardowns of 5 kids apps + 1 adult reference → HeyLolo onboarding+paywall spec v2 + rejected dark patterns. Research wave 2026-07-05 · lean brief (~14 web sources). Builds on day0-activation-playbook.md (Day-0 flow) and SYNTHESIS-WINNING-PLAYBOOK.md (strategy) — does not repeat them. This doc is the funnel-mechanics layer: taps-to-value, paywall placement, trial framing, price anchoring.

Method & caveat

Public teardown blogs (Adapty, Purchasely, PaywallPro, RevenueCat, growth/UX case studies), app-store + help-center pages, and parent-review sites. Kids-app funnels are under-documented screen-by-screen (most teardown libraries cover fitness/AI), so a few sequences are reconstructed from help-center + review evidence, flagged where inferred. Prices are US $ as listed 2025–26 and drift.

1. Lingokids — the subscription-conversion machine

Ages 2–8, ~$3M/mo revenue, 500k+ monthly downloads (Adapty). The category's best funnel.

Step sequence (taps to first kid moment ≈ 3–4):

  1. Splash → playable demo immediately — the child taps into a game/activity within seconds; no signup gate first. Value-before-ask.
  2. Short personalization quiz (child's age → tailors content). Only questions that change the plan.
  3. Parent-gate (math/hold gate) protects the account/purchase area from the kid.
  4. Paywall — appears after the child has already played, so the parent is deciding on a product they've watched work.

Paywall / trial: 7-day free trial. Annual plan is the hero, shown with a per-month breakdown of the annual price so the big number is reframed as "$X/month." Monthly shown as the expensive anchor beside it. Bold cartoon characters + brand-trust (Oxford University Press association) on the screen. In-app add-on packs $14.99–$89.99; core annual ~$71.99–$89.99, monthly ~$14.99–$15.99.

Objections pre-empted: "will my kid actually use it?" (they already played) · "is it educational/legit?" (Oxford badge) · "am I trapped?" ("easy cancellation" copy).

Done badly: the parent gate + immediate paywall after a fun demo reads to some parents as bait ("let the kid get hooked, then charge me"). Add-on IAP stack up to $89.99 muddies the "one subscription" story.

2. Duolingo ABC — best-funded kids onboarding

Ages 3–6, literacy/phonics. Notably: free, no ads, no IAP (funded by the main Duolingo business). So it is the reference for onboarding craft without a paywall, not for monetization.

Step sequence: open → pick child/age → straight into a lesson. Almost zero setup friction because there's nothing to sell. Onboarding and lesson code are deliberately separate modules (Duolingo eng blog). For kids specifically, Duolingo stripped streaks and leagues (loss-aversion mechanics) — validating our no-loss stance.

Trial / price: none (free).

Objections pre-empted: "safe for a 4-year-old?" — no ads, no purchases, walled garden is the entire pitch.

Done badly: limited scope (early literacy only), and being free means no urgency signal — parents don't value it as highly. Not a conversion template; a craft + safety template.

Takeaway for us: the child-facing flow must be a walled garden with zero purchase surface visible to the kid (see §6 FTC). The selling happens on a separate parent surface.

3. Pok Pok — the closest comparable (subscription-only + trial)

Ages 2–7, Apple Design Award "digital playroom," Montessori open-ended play. Subscription-only, ad-free, explicitly no manipulative design — the brand parents trust.

Step sequence: download → 7-day free trial starts → child plays the full playroom (all toys unlocked during trial) → after 7 days the chosen plan auto-starts and bills. Value is experienced before any charge; the trial is the onboarding.

Trial / price: 7-day free trial on both plans (card required via App Store). Monthly $6.99; annual $45.99 (~$3.83/mo). A lifetime deal exists as a separate promo (StackSocial). Annual is the value anchor against monthly.

Objections pre-empted: the core pitch is anti-dark-pattern — "hand it over without worrying about ads, pressure, or manipulative design" (Cubby review). No ads, no pop-ups, no upsell to the child. Relentless A/B testing of messaging/value-prop/video on the paywall (RevenueCat).

Done badly: card-required trial → the standard "forgot to cancel, got billed" complaint pattern (inherent to App-Store trials). Open-ended play with no goals means some parents can't articulate the ROI vs a curriculum app.

Why it's our closest comp: premium, ad-free, trust-first, subscription-only, character/toy-led. Their positioning ("no manipulative design") is exactly our brand guardrail — we should out-clean them by going no-card.

4. Epic! — the classic reading trial→annual funnel

40,000+ books; the mass-market reading subscription. Up to 4 child profiles.

Step sequence: signup (email/account up front — heavier gate than the kids apps above) → set up child profiles (up to 4) → instant catalog access → hits the trial/paywall quickly. Also runs an ad-supported "Epic Free" (2 hrs/week) as a permanent limited tier / soft funnel.

Trial / price: 7-day free trial → monthly $11.99–$13.99 vs annual $79.99–$84.99 (≈$7.08/mo). Annual framed as the ~40% saving. Multi-child (4 profiles) is a value multiplier on one price.

Objections pre-empted: "enough content?" (40k books) · "for all my kids?" (4 profiles, one price) · "try before paying?" (free tier + trial).

Done badly — the cautionary tale: reviews + FTC context flag "very easy to sign up, but very hard to cancel." Epic's parent-company (Epic Games, separate entity but same reputational category) and ABCmouse (direct competitor) are the FTC's headline cases for promising "Easy Cancellation" then trapping users — the single reputational landmine for a kids subscription. Frictional cancel = the pattern we must visibly avoid.

5. Moshi — calm onboarding (sleep)

Ages 0–12, sleep/meditation, 1,000+ stories. 67% daily-use in our SYNTHESIS notes; "kids sleep 28 min faster" science claim.

Step sequence: open → gentle, low-stimulation onboarding (recently A/B-tested a new onboarding + home) → sample content → paywall. Calm, quiet visual tone matches the product (sleep), the opposite of Lingokids' loud carousel.

Trial / price: 7-day free trial → annual $59.99/yr vs monthly $9.99. Annual hero; monthly is the anchor that makes $59.99 (≈$5/mo) look reasonable. Science/award trust cues on screen.

Objections pre-empted: "does it work?" (28-min sleep science + awards) · "age range?" (0–12 span = long LTV).

Done badly: repeated review complaint that the trial sign-up implies a monthly subscription but actually converts to annual — "the subscription language is clear, but it appears not to be." That ambiguity is a trust tax and a chargeback/refund driver. Our anti-pattern to name: never let trial→plan framing be ambiguous.

Takeaway: Moshi's $59.99/yr annual hero is the exact price point our settled canon adopts — validated by a same-category, same-audience premium app.

6. Duolingo (adult) — the "invest-then-ask" reference

The canonical sequence-commitment-before-paywall pattern.

Mechanic: product first, account/paywall last. New user does a translation/lesson exercise before any signup → only after finishing does a dismissible soft-wall ask them to create a profile + pick a small daily goal (a commitment device) → a hard wall appears only after several lessons. Moving signup back a few steps produced ~+20% DAUs / sign-ups. The paywall itself: 3-part value carousel before price, annual default/"most popular," then post-purchase screens re-selling the 5 key benefits ("aftercare").

Why it matters for us: commitment is manufactured by investment, not by demanding the card early. Every step of value experienced before the ask raises conversion and lowers cancels. This is the backbone of our spec: kid's first LoLo moment happens before the parent ever sees a price.

The dark edge to avoid: Duolingo's 2025 "pushy paywall" push (Motley Fool) monetizes harder with aggressive interstitials — profitable for a viral adult app, corrosive for a kids-safety brand. We take the sequence, not the pushiness.

B. HeyLolo onboarding + paywall spec v2 (deltas to current code)

Builds on the #178 4-step trim already specced in day0-activation-playbook.md §1/§3 (general → avatar → interests → handoffPrimer). This adds where the trial screen sits and its copy, plus the price-page layout.

B1. Flow order — paywall AFTER the first kid moment, not before. Current OnboardingSteps (src/features/onboarding/model.ts:32) is an 8-step form ending at pinCode; subscription is a separate widget. Sequence must be: general → avatar → interests → **handoffPrimer** → [KID'S FIRST LOLO MOMENT + first wonder] → **Trial screen** (parent) → dashboard. The trial/paywall is the first thing the parent sees after watching the kid's first wonder land — the Duolingo invest-then-ask position. Never show price before the aha. (Deferred config — PIN, quiet-hours, emergency-contact, phone, custom-interests, support-areas, chat-style — moves to a post-first-session "Finish setting up" dashboard card, per §3 of the playbook.)

B2. Trial screen sits here, and says this (EN copy):

14 days free. No card. No catch. LoLo is all yours for two weeks — every story, every wonder, every voice chat. We won't ask for a card until you decide to stay. (primary) Start my 14 free days (secondary, quiet) Not now — keep exploring ↓ fine print: On day 14 we'll email you first. Keep it for $59.99/year (≈$5/mo) or do nothing and it simply ends. Cancel in two taps, anytime.

Rationale vs the field: no-card removes Pok Pok/Epic's "forgot to cancel → billed" trap; "we'll email you first / it simply ends" is the explicit anti-Moshi clarity; "cancel in two taps" is the visible anti-Epic/ABCmouse promise (and we must make it true — §C).

B3. Price-page layout (annual hero $59.99): Current pricing (src/widgets/subscription/model/pricing.ts) prices Adventurer at $14.99/mo → ~$143.90/yr (20% off 12mo). Delta: re-anchor to the settled canon.

  • Annual is the hero card, pre-selected, badge "Best Value" (already the Adventurer badge, plan-content.ts:24): $59.99/year, subtitle "just $4.99/month, billed yearly." (The ÷12 reframe every competitor uses.)
  • Monthly is the anchor beside it, visibly worse: $9.99/month → "$119.88/year." Not pre-selected. (Matches Moshi's $59.99/$9.99 exactly.)
  • Family framing on the hero: "one price · up to 4 kids" (per SYNTHESIS standing validation — multi-profile is shipped; Epic's 4-profile lever).
  • Trust row under the cards: "Made in EU · your child's data stays private · no ads, ever · no in-app purchases the kid can tap" (our differentiators; directly answers the FTC unauthorized-charge fear).
  • Delta on Explorer copy: plan-content.ts:39 says the trial is "30 days" — must read 14 days to match canon. Reconcile the trial length in one place.

Three biggest concrete changes: (1) move the paywall to after the kid's first wonder (invest-then-ask) — not a pre-value gate; (2) re-price the annual hero to $59.99/yr with a $9.99/mo anchor and the ÷12 "$4.99/mo" reframe (current code is $14.99/mo → fix pricing.ts); (3) no-card trial screen with explicit "we'll email you, it just ends, cancel in two taps" copy (fix the 30-day→14-day drift in plan-content.ts).

C. The 5 conversion tactics we deliberately REJECT (with what we do instead)

  1. Card-required "free" trial that auto-bills silently. (Pok Pok/Epic/App-Store default; the ABCmouse FTC case.) → No-card 14-day trial; we email before it ends and it lapses to nothing unless the parent opts in. Costs some auto-conversions, buys trust + zero refund/chargeback risk.
  2. Trial→plan framing ambiguity (implying monthly, billing annual). (Moshi's live review complaint.) → State the exact plan, price, and date on the trial screen ("Keep it for $59.99/year on day 14, or it ends"). One unambiguous sentence.
  3. Cancellation friction / hidden cancel. (Epic "hard to cancel"; ABCmouse "Easy Cancellation" lie → FTC action.) → Two-tap cancel from the dashboard, surfaced in onboarding copy, and honor it. Our cancel path must be shorter than our subscribe path.
  4. Any purchase surface or upsell visible to the child. (Amazon/Epic Games unauthorized-charge cases; FTC + COPPA.) → All commerce lives behind the parent gate; the kid's world is a walled garden (the Duolingo ABC standard). No "ask a grown-up to unlock" nag aimed at the child, no IAP the kid can tap.
  5. Loss-aversion pressure aimed at the child — streaks-you-can-lose, countdown timers, "your friend LoLo is sad you left," aggressive interstitials. (Duolingo adult's 2025 "pushy paywall"; general engagement dark patterns.) → No-loss journey + promise-of-delight (Tomorrow's Seed, per SYNTHESIS #157). Urgency, if any, lives only on the parent's trial screen ("day 14"), never on the kid.
