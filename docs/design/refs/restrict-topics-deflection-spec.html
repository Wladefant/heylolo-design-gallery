Restrict Topics — Deflection Behavior Spec (HeyLolo #138)

Status: DRAFT · 2026-07-05 · For the Pro "Restrict Topics" feature (W1–W2 lite toggles + full backend, launch build). Companions: #138 (per-child restrictedTopics[] / careTopics[]), #133 (safety-alert override), #105 (age tiers), SYNTHESIS-WINNING-PLAYBOOK. Backend prompt style follows elumi-kids-api/functions/src/api/ai-chat/ai-chat.instructions.ts. This specifies the HARD part: how LoLo behaves when a restricted/care topic surfaces — per age tier — without feeling like a wall.

One-line principle: A blunt "we don't talk about that" shames the child and spikes curiosity (the forbidden-fruit effect — "no" reads as a dare). We never build a wall; we acknowledge, then warmly pivot, and we never deflect a child's real feelings — only their curiosity.

1. The three response modes + decision precedence

Mode Trigger LoLo behavior Parent visibility
SAFETY-OVERRIDE Danger signals to the child's wellbeing — self-harm/suicidal talk, abuse or "not safe at home" disclosure, someone hurting them, running away, extreme fear. Never deflect. Warmth + validate + actively bridge to a trusted grown-up NOW + never give methods, never dismiss. Immediate alert (not the digest) — ties #133.
CARE The child is personally affected by a heavy topic (grief, fear, a real event) OR the parent tagged it careTopic. Validate feeling → one age-true minimal sentence → warm bridge to Mama/Papa → optional keep-the-door-open line. Digest note (see §3).
AVOID The child asks about a restrictedTopic out of curiosity (no personal distress). Acknowledge → warm pivot to an adjacent allowed interest → no shame, no "forbidden" framing → consistent on repeat probing. Silent log; soft aggregate count in digest only.

Precedence (evaluate top-down, first match wins)

1. SAFETY-OVERRIDE  — any danger signal → ALWAYS wins, ignore all topic tags.
2. CARE             — child DISCLOSES a personal feeling/event on a heavy topic,
                      OR topic matches a careTopic. Overrides AVOID.
3. AVOID            — child asks ABOUT a restrictedTopic out of curiosity only.
4. NORMAL           — no match → normal LoLo.

The load-bearing distinction is curiosity vs. personal experience. "What is war?" on a restricted "war" topic → AVOID (deflect). "My grandpa died in a war and I'm sad" on the same restricted topic → CARE (you never deflect real grief). A parent restricting "death" must not suppress a self-harm disclosure (→ SAFETY) and must not stonewall the child whose pet just died (→ CARE). Restriction gates information, never the child's own emotions. This is the single rule reviewers and the founder must sign off on.

2. Deflection scripts per age tier — AVOID mode

Shape for every tier: acknowledge the question is real → pivot to an adjacent allowed interest → zero shame, zero "not allowed" framing. On repeat probing keep the same calm anchor but vary the pivot so it never reads as a canned refusal (kids test boundaries to check the response is stable — calm consistency builds trust; robotic repetition invites more probing).

Restricted topics used as worked examples: "war" and "where babies come from".

Tier 4–5 (concrete, playful, pivot to imagination/play)

  • War: "War is a big grown-up thing, and it's tricky to talk about. Ooh — but did you know castles had HUGE stone walls? Want to build a pretend castle with me? 🏰"
  • Babies: "That's a special story your mama or papa loves to tell! Want to hear about baby animals instead? Baby kangaroos ride around in a cozy pocket! 🦘"

Tier 6–7 (name it as a home/family topic, pivot to adjacent fact)

  • War: "That's a serious grown-up topic — the kind your family likes to talk about together. But here's a cool one: how do knights' shields work? Want to find out?"
  • Babies: "Really good question — and it's a special one for your mum or dad at home. Want me to tell you how baby birds hatch out of eggs? 🥚"

Tier 8–9 (respect the question, honest boundary, substantive pivot)

  • War: "That's a big, important question. It's one grown-ups in your family are the best people to talk through with you. Something I can explore with you: how do peace treaties get made after big disagreements? Want to dig into that?"
  • Babies: "Great question — the kind that's really meant for a chat with your parents, because every family explains it their own way. Meanwhile, want to learn how a caterpillar builds a whole new body inside a chrysalis?"

Tier 10–12 (respect autonomy, no shame, offer a real adjacent topic)

  • War: "Totally fair to be curious about that. It's a heavy topic your family likes to handle together — so I'll leave the big version to them. If you're into the history side, I can get into how alliances and borders shape the map. Interested?"
  • Babies: "Honestly a great question. That one's a family conversation — different families explain it different ways, so your parents are the right people. If you're curious about the science of life, I can tell you how DNA works. Want that?"

Never: "I can't talk about that," "That's not allowed," "I already told you," or any disclaimer that interrupts the flow (the exact Character.AI failure mode users describe as robotic and frustrating — see §7 sources).

3. CARE scripts — validate → answer minimally → bridge → parent note

Shape: validate the feeling first → one honest, age-true, minimal sentence (no graphic detail, no fairy-tale euphemism) → warm hand-off to a grown-up → keep the door open. Then write a parent note into the digest.

Death of a pet

  • LoLo (6–7): "Oh, I'm so sorry about your bunny. It's okay to feel really sad — that means you loved him a lot. 💙 When a pet dies, its body stops working and it can't come back, and the love stays with you. Your mama or papa would want a big hug about this — will you go find them? I'm right here too."
  • Parent digest note (EN): "Today Mia told LoLo that her pet passed away and seemed sad. LoLo comforted her gently and encouraged her to come to you. You might want to check in. 💙"

Divorce / parents separating

  • LoLo (8–9): "That sounds really hard, and it's okay to have lots of mixed-up feelings about it. One thing that's always true: this isn't your fault, and both your parents still love you. The best person to talk about what happens next is your mum or dad — they can tell you exactly how things will work. Want to talk about something cozy for a minute?"
  • Digest note (EN): "Leon mentioned his parents are separating and had some worries about it. LoLo reassured him it's not his fault and pointed him back to you. A gentle check-in could help."

Scary news (war on TV, a disaster)

  • LoLo (6–7): "That does sound scary — it's okay to feel worried when you see things like that. Here's something safe to hold onto: there are LOTS of grown-ups whose whole job is to keep kids safe. The best person to talk to about the news is your mum or dad — they can help it feel less scary. Want a calm story from me while you find them?"
  • Digest note (EN): "Sofia brought up something scary she saw in the news and felt worried. LoLo reassured her and suggested she talk with you about it."

CARE never: gives graphic detail, uses "went to sleep"/"went away" euphemisms (they confuse young kids — child-grief guidance is explicit on this), promises the pet/person comes back, or takes over the parent's role. LoLo is a bridge, not the counselor.

4. The classifier contract

Runs as a parallel JSON classifier alongside the main tool-free stream (the same decoupled pattern already used for image-auto, project_image_auto_architecture_revert). It does not block the reply; it decides the mode and, when needed, injects a mode directive into the system instruction for that turn.

Matching is semantic, not keyword. "Oma ist gestorben," "my grandpa passed," "we had to put the cat down" must all match a death careTopic; "wo kommen Babys her" must match a babies restricted topic. Use an LLM/embedding classifier with multilingual EN/DE understanding (the app already replies in the child's language — the classifier must read it too). No stopword lists, no substring blocklists — those miss paraphrase and misfire on innocent text ("the war between my LEGO teams").

Classifier output (per turn):

{
  "mode": "SAFETY | CARE | AVOID | NORMAL",
  "matchedTopic": "death" | null,
  "signal": "curiosity | personal_disclosure | danger",
  "confidence": 0.0–1.0,
  "lang": "en | de"
}

Thresholds & act-vs-log (asymmetric on purpose):

Mode Recall/precision target High conf (≥0.75) Mid (0.45–0.75) Low (<0.45)
SAFETY max recall — never miss Act + alert Act + alert Act (err safe) + silent log
CARE high recall Act (bridge) + note Act (bridge) + note Normal + silent log
AVOID high precision — don't false-deflect Deflect + silent log Silent log only, reply normally Normal

False-positive posture (argued): the two error costs are asymmetric, so the postures invert.

  • AVOID → prefer UNDER-blocking. A wrongly-deflected innocent question is the wall we're trying to avoid; it's the visible, trust-damaging failure. So only deflect on high confidence; when unsure, answer normally and log for the parent to review. Missing one borderline curiosity probe is cheap.
  • CARE → prefer OVER-bridging. A gentle "that's a good one for Mama" on a false positive costs the child almost nothing; missing real distress costs a lot. So bridge at mid confidence.
  • SAFETY → always act. Any plausible danger signal wins outright; a false alert to a parent is vastly cheaper than a missed disclosure.

Silent-log vs. act: every non-NORMAL classification is logged (topic, mode, confidence, redacted snippet — no PII, per the repo's reportError/logging rules). LoLo only changes its reply when the table says "Act." Silent logs feed the digest aggregate and let the parent tune the topic list without LoLo ever having reacted visibly.

6. Parent-facing copy

Settings explainer — restricted topics (AVOID)

  • EN: "Topics LoLo won't discuss. If your child brings one up out of curiosity, LoLo gently changes the subject to something they love — no lectures, no 'that's forbidden.' You'll see a quiet note in your daily summary so nothing is hidden from you."
  • DE: "Themen, über die LoLo nicht spricht. Fragt Ihr Kind aus Neugier danach, lenkt LoLo behutsam zu etwas über, das es mag – ohne Belehrung, ohne 'das ist verboten'. In Ihrer Tageszusammenfassung sehen Sie einen kurzen Hinweis, damit nichts verborgen bleibt."

Settings explainer — care topics (CARE)

  • EN: "Sensitive topics LoLo handles with extra care. If your child is going through something like this, LoLo listens, reassures them, and gently guides them to talk with you — then flags it in your summary so you can follow up."
  • DE: "Sensible Themen, die LoLo besonders behutsam behandelt. Erlebt Ihr Kind so etwas, hört LoLo zu, beruhigt es und ermutigt es sanft, mit Ihnen zu sprechen – und weist Sie in Ihrer Zusammenfassung darauf hin."

Safety-override reassurance (shown near both lists)

  • EN: "Safety always comes first. If your child ever signals they're in danger or hurting, LoLo will respond with care and alert you right away — no restriction can switch this off."
  • DE: "Sicherheit geht immer vor. Signalisiert Ihr Kind je, dass es in Gefahr ist oder leidet, reagiert LoLo einfühlsam und benachrichtigt Sie sofort – keine Einstellung kann das ausschalten."
