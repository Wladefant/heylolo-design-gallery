PART 1 — Research synthesis (psychology + regulation + teardowns)

1.1 Gamification psychology for ages 4–10

The single most important finding for this age band, from GAMIFICATION-SCIENCE-KIDS.md:

  • The standard Points-Badges-Leaderboards (PBL) stack has its WEAKEST evidence exactly in HeyLoLo's age band. Kurnaz (2025) meta-analysis (k=41): primary-school effect g = 0.309 vs secondary g = 1.015; and gamification moved extrinsic motivation (g = 0.713) more than intrinsic (g = 0.638). PBL raises felt autonomy/relatedness but does not reliably raise felt competence (ETR&D 2023). https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/pits.70056
  • Overjustification is worse for children than adults. Deci, Koestner & Ryan (1999, 128 studies): "tangible rewards tended to be more detrimental for children than college students, and verbal rewards tend to be less enhancing for children." Danner & Lonky (1981, ages 4–10): rewards decreased intrinsic motivation among the already-motivated children. The founding study — Lepper, Greene & Nisbett (1973) — cut preschoolers' voluntary engagement with a loved activity by ~50% once the promised reward stopped, and the kids with the highest intrinsic interest were the most easily undermined. Design implication: rewards for a companion kids already enjoy should be informational (signal competence) and/or unexpected/surprise — not promised, contingent payouts. (Full cites in WEB-RESEARCH.md.) Children are the worst population for reward-driven design on both sides. https://home.ubalt.edu/tmitch/642/articles%20syllabus/Deci%20Koestner%20Ryan%20meta%20IM%20psy%20bull%2099.pdf
  • Competence at 4–6 = "I did it / I caused it," not a score. Nicholls' developmental gradient: at 5–6 effort and ability are undifferentiated; social comparison / normative feedback only starts to bite around first grade. Leaderboards are developmentally meaningless for 4–6-year-olds and actively discouraging for the 8–9s who do compare and can be shamed by normative failure. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2268096/
  • Intrinsic integration beats bolt-on rewards. Habgood & Ainsworth (2011): children learned more AND chose to play 7× longer when the learning was woven into the core mechanic vs. a "quiz-then-treat" bolt-on. → make the loop itself the reward. https://shura.shu.ac.uk/3556/1/Habgood_Ainsworth_final.pdf
  • Collections behave differently (and better) than points. Collecting is a normative middle-childhood identity activity; collectors report higher personal control/self-efficacy. Collections are concrete, visible, narratively meaningful, and complete-able — they have a natural endpoint. Points are abstract, infinite, comparative. (D4CR "balanced environment" asks precisely for finite reward loops.)
  • Streaks under 10 = an evidence vacuum filled by industry hype. There is essentially no peer-reviewed research on daily-streak mechanics in children under 10. What exists is adolescent loss-aversion critique ("the emotional weight shifts from pleasure of connection to fear of loss") and parent reports that Duolingo streaks morph into "genuine anxiety and performative learning." Young kids can't control their own daily schedule (parents do) and are self-blame-prone. Verdict from the science dossier: calendar streaks are unjustifiable for 4–9s on the evidence. https://screenwiseapp.com/guides/duolingo-streaks-and-anxiety-in-kids | shame-free-streak design: https://uxmag.com/articles/the-psychology-of-hot-streak-game-design-how-to-keep-players-coming-back-every-day-without-shame
  • Praise = process, honest intensity. Mueller & Dweck (1998): one line of ability praise reduced persistence and post-failure performance. Brummelman (2014/2017): inflated praise ("AMAZING! INCREDIBLE!") harms low-confidence kids most — the exact kids most likely to receive it. Celebrate the doing/strategy, never "you're so smart," never intensifier-stacking. https://www.columbia.edu/cu/psychology/courses/3615/Readings/Mueller_Dweck.pdf
  • Celebration arousal arc: build → peak (≤2–3 s) → settle. Lillard & Peterson (2011): 9 minutes of fast-paced TV "severely compromised" 4-year-olds' executive function. Takacs (2015): content-congruent multimedia helps, but extra interactive juice overloads (worst for disadvantaged kids). → celebrations that are about the content help; garnish costs learning. Model regulation, don't spike-and-cut. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4647204/
  • UNICEF RITEC-8 is the positive design standard (autonomy, competence, relationships = the SDT needs, + safety, emotions, creativity, identity). Audit every mechanic against it. https://digitalthrivingplaybook.org/big-idea/designing-for-childrens-well-being-in-digital-play-the-ritec-8-framework/

1.2 What real kids apps actually ship (teardowns)

From TEARDOWNS-PREMIUM-KIDS-APPS.md + WEB-RESEARCH.md:

  • Duolingo ABC (ages 3–8): deliberately removed all leagues, all social features, all leaderboards, ads, IAP — "main-Duolingo's social features and League competition are too much" for this age. Kept: single linear path, star-earning that unlocks stories, micro/inline celebrations (Duo mascot mid-exercise, "letters snapping into place," "a generous dose of sparkles"). Celebrations are inline, never blocking modals. https://blog.duolingo.com/a-good-read-building-duolingo-abc-for-android/
  • Khan Academy Kids: celebration = generosity, not score — correct answers earn sparkles that accumulate to bring a truck of presents for the characters (not the child); Kodi the bear celebrates with the child and models thinking during struggle; wrong answers never punish (gentle "bong" + animated walkthrough). Longer-term reward = furnishings for characters' rooms — a collection, given to the character. https://blog.khanacademy.org/supporting-english-language-acquisition-with-khan-academy-kids/
  • Toca Boca / Sago Mini / Pok Pok: the anti-gamification flagships — "these are not games, they are toys… no feelings of failure"; "no high scores, no Game Over, zero stress." Apple Design Award 2026 to Sago Mini: "remarkable for what it doesn't include… no timer, no to-do lists." Pok Pok: "there's no flashing 'congratulations'… deeper play IS the reward."
  • Pokémon Smile (toothbrushing): the closest mainstream example of ethical extrinsic reward for young kids — brushing "catches" Pokémon (a collection with a natural set to complete) and earns cosmetic hats; no streak-loss punishment, no leaderboard, no social. It's a collection loop wrapped around a real-world habit. (See WEB-RESEARCH.md for the sourced teardown.)
  • Apple Activity Rings (transferable adult pattern): endurance from radical simplicity — three loops, sparkle-burst on close, scaled celebration for milestone awards.

The convergent lesson: premium kids gamification is subtraction + collection + character-delivered celebration, NOT competitive PBL.

1.3 COPPA + Apple Kids Category — the leaderboard/social verdict

Full detail + the 20-row compliance checklist in REGULATORY-GUARDRAILS.md. The load-bearing constraints for a leaderboard / "Challenge Friends":

  • Apple Guideline 1.3 (Kids Category, verbatim): Kids-Category apps "may not send personally identifiable information or device information to third parties" and must keep behind a parental gate any links out, purchasing, or "other distractions." A public/social leaderboard that shows one child's name+score to another child is a disclosure surface Apple scrutinises heavily; UGC/social (Guideline 1.2) then also attaches (filtering, reporting, blocking, published contact).
  • Apple 5.1.4(b): the parental gate is NOT the same as COPPA parental consent — you need both.
  • COPPA amended Rule (compliance deadline already passed 2026-04-22): displaying a child's personal info to other users, and collecting friend lists, are exactly the disclosures that require separate opt-in VPC. A "Have 10 friends" incentive that drives kids to accumulate connections is a friend-list-collection engine. FTC v. Epic ($275M) established that on-by-default social matching of kids with others is unlawful and that defaults are design decisions the FTC treats as unfair.
  • EU DSA Art. 28(1) Guidelines (14 Jul 2025) name, for minors, features that must be OFF by default: "communication 'streaks'… likes/reaction counts… intermittent or random rewards." Public leaderboards are engagement-comparison surfaces this regime pushes away from.
  • EU AI Act Art. 5(1)(b) — the only outright ban: an AI that exploits a child's age-vulnerability to distort behaviour (effect suffices, no intent needed). A companion that leverages the parasocial bond or peer-comparison for engagement sits closest to this red line.

COPPA/Apple leaderboard verdict (verified — matches the expected safe answer): a global or friends-of-strangers leaderboard is not shippable for this audience. The only compliant shapes are:

  1. Self-competition ("you vs. your own best / last week") — always safe, no other person's data shown.
  2. Family-scoped, inside a single parent-owned account/household, where every member (child + parents + siblings) is already known to each other and the parent controls membership — no cross-household disclosure, no stranger discovery, no friend-request graph. This is the shape the Figma design gestures at (it lists "Mom", "Dad", "Grandpa Sam" beside the kids) but it currently also mixes in non-family kid names, which is the part to cut.
  3. Fully anonymized/aggregate ("you're in the top 20% of curious kids this week") — no names, no identifiable others.

Everything social-graph (friend requests, "Challenge Friends" across households, "Have N friends" badges) must be gated behind the F30 safety audit + VPC + a parental gate, and is a Pillar-5 (parent/safety) decision, not a launch gamification feature. The catalog itself sequences F27/F31 (social) after F30 (CATALOG.md build-order).

Consent-method trap (WEB-RESEARCH.md): the low-friction "email-plus" VPC method is only valid when the operator does NOT disclose the child's PI to third parties or make it publicly available — which legally forbids public leaderboards, third-party analytics, and social APIs. So a public/friends leaderboard doesn't just need some consent; it forces you off email-plus onto a higher-friction VPC method (credit card, gov-ID+face-match, KBA). Another reason the launch answer is self + family-scoped only.

1.4 Anti-addiction / healthy-use guardrails (design rules)

Newest policy anchors (WEB-RESEARCH.md): AAP policy "Digital Ecosystems, Children, and Adolescents" (Jan 20, 2026) explicitly flags intermittent variable-reward systems, endless scroll, and notification spam as compulsive-use harms — so the classic variable-ratio "slot-machine" schedule is exactly what to avoid; AAP's endorsed lane is "high-quality, low-gamification." UK ICO Children's Code Standard 13 prohibits nudge techniques / dark patterns (no FOMO countdowns, no loss-aversion pressure, no streak guilt).

Distilled from all dossiers into hard rules the reward economy must obey:

  • No calendar streaks with loss framing (fire icons, "don't break your streak"). DSA names streaks for default-off; the science says no efficacy under 10 + self-blame risk.
  • No intermittent/random rewards (mystery chests, gacha, loot-box-like surprise) — the compulsion engine; FTC v. Cognosphere/Genshin ($20M) is the enforcement template. Fixed, transparent reward rules; variability only in content (which sticker, which sound), never in whether effort is acknowledged.
  • No FOMO/countdown/urgency cues in the kid UI.
  • The character never emotionally pressures use ("I'll miss you", "don't leave me") — closest to the EU AI Act ban.
  • No child-directed engagement push notifications by default; route "come back" to the parent.
  • Treat any token/point system as scaffolding to fade (ABA arc: continuous → intermittent → natural consequence). If the behaviour collapses when the reward is removed, the design failed.
  • Every gamification lever behind a parent-dashboard toggle, conservative by default — "off by default, parent opts in" is the pattern every regime converges on (DSA, AADC, COPPA, Epic order).

1.5 Reward-economy design principles

  • Size XP so no single loop dominates — budget by effort + learning value, not by which loop is cheapest to spam. A 1-tap coloring fill must not out-earn a completed story. Use a small, legible currency scale and daily soft-caps per activity class so a child can't farm one mechanic.
  • Level curve: gentle, front-loaded (fast early levels so a 4-year-old feels progress in session one), then a mild slope. Avoid steep exponential curves that make later levels feel unreachable (anxiety). Levels should be cosmetic/identity milestones, never a gate on content.
  • Badge taxonomy = three families: (a) skill-milestone (first story, first quiz-all-correct), (b) collection-completeness (collect the 8 "explorer critters"), (c) effort/consistency (came back and learned across several days — framed as an ever-advancing map, never a losable streak). Keep active collections capped and completable.
  • Streak-safe recovery: if any continuity feature ships at all, it must be a no-loss "adventure map" that only ever advances — gaps are invisible, returning is always celebrated, never mourned. If a Duolingo-style streak is insisted on, it must ship with streak-freeze / weekend-amulet equivalents auto-granted and be parent-toggleable + off by default.

PART 4 — Recommended system for elumi-kids

Design thesis: the research is unambiguous that competitive PBL is the weakest and riskiest choice for 4–10s, yet the Figma design (and the "super/leaderboard/Challenge-Friends" ask) points at PBL. Resolve this by shipping a collection-first, self-referential, family-scoped system that looks rich but avoids every harm/compliance red line — and by making XP a private progress signal, not a public ranking. This also matches what the codebase is already built for (the unwired success("L") / playLandmarkPattern() "collection-shelf moment").

4.1 XP event taxonomy — one currency, effort-weighted, daily-capped

Currency: a single earned currency, Curiosity Points (XP). No second spendable "coins" currency at launch (avoids store/IAP/loot-box scrutiny). XP is private to the child + visible to the parent; it drives the child's own level + collection, and (optionally) a family-only board.

Grant table (illustrative weights — tune once F2 age tiers land so a T1 4-year-old and a T4 tween earn comparably per unit of effort). Weight by effort + learning value, with per-class daily soft-caps so no loop can be farmed:

Source feature Event XP Daily soft-cap
Chat (ai-chat-messages) Ask a genuine question (dedup trivial spam) 5 ~40 (8 questions)
Chat "Today's 3 curious questions" goal complete (existing TodaysGoalCard) +15 bonus once/day
Explorer (F1) "LoLo sees it!" object identified 5 ~30
Learning game (F6–F19) Complete a round/level 10 ~60 across all games
Learning game All-correct / personal-best round +10 bonus —
Quiz (F16/F18) Finish a quiz 10; +2/correct ~40
Story/comic (F23) Finish a co-created book 30 ~60
Coloring (F24) Finish (meaningfully) a page 10 ~30
Cooking/plant (F20/F21) Complete a recipe / daily plant-care 10 10 (care once/day)
Vocabulary (F9) SRS review batch done 10 ~30
Consistency Returned + learned on a new day (no-loss) +10 once/day

Guardrails baked in: fixed/transparent grants (no random XP), per-class caps prevent single-loop dominance, and the total daily earnable is bounded so a healthy ~15-min session tops out — the reward economy cannot itself pull for overuse (REGULATORY §7 rows 19–20; interacts cleanly with the existing timeManagement limits).

4.2 Levels

  • "Explorer Levels" 1…N, cosmetic/identity only — never gate content or features behind a level (would fail Apple 1.3 "distraction" + the autonomy principle).
  • Curve: front-loaded — L1→L2 in one short session; then a gentle near-linear slope (e.g. xpForLevel(n) = 50 + 40·(n−1)), so progress is always felt and no level looks unreachable. Level-up fires feedback.playLandmarkPattern() + an avatar (F26) celebration, then settles (arousal arc).
  • Each level unlocks a cosmetic (an avatar hat/sticker for the LoLo cloud, matching the Figma costumed-cloud language) — cosmetic-only rewards are the ethical form (Pokémon Smile model).

4.3 Badge families (three, plus the launch badge list)

  1. Skill-milestone (one-off firsts): First Question, First Story, First All-Correct Quiz, First Coloring, First Explorer Find. Warm, effort-framed titles.
  2. Collection-completeness (the primary engine — collections beat points for this age): themed sticker/critter sets with a finite, completable set (e.g. "8 Curiosity Critters," "6 Space Explorers"). Earned across heterogeneous features so the whole app feeds one shelf — this is the "deeply integrated" spine. Completing a set closes an arc and fires the big celebration; it does not open an infinite bigger meter.
  3. Consistency (no-loss): framed as an ever-advancing adventure map, never a losable streak. "You've explored on 5 different days!" — gaps invisible, returning always celebrated.

Reuse the Figma badge cards + locked-state UI. Cut "Have 10 friends" at launch (COPPA). Keep the rest (Curious Cloud, Quiz Champion, Why Machine, Rising Writer, Tiny Picasso) — they're clean activity-milestones.

4.4 Streak design (the riskiest decision — see §4.9)

  • Do NOT escalate the existing hard-reset learningStreakDays into a fire-icon loseable streak. Keep it as the current descriptive "N-day learning streak" label, OR replace with the no-loss adventure map.
  • If a visible streak is a hard product requirement: ship it off by default, parent-toggleable, with auto-granted streak-freezes / weekend-amulets, no fire/guilt iconography, and "resume, never mourned" framing (DSA default-off; the shame-free-streak UX literature).

4.5 Leaderboard scope — recommendation (COPPA verdict)

Ship, in priority order:

  1. Self-board (default, always on): "You vs. your own best week / last week" + level progress. No other person's data — always compliant, and it's what actually motivates the 4–6 competence need ("I did more than before").
  2. Family board (opt-in, parent-provisioned): a single-household board across the parent-owned account (child + parents + siblings), exactly the Mom/Dad/Grandpa shape in the Figma — membership controlled by the parent, no cross-household discovery, no friend graph, behind a parental gate. This is the only compliant multi-person leaderboard.
  3. Anonymous aggregate (optional): "top 20% of curious kids this week" — no names.

Do NOT ship: global leaderboards, friends-of-strangers boards, or a cross-household "Challenge Friends" at launch — not COPPA-shippable, and it depends on F31 (gated behind F30). Reframe "Challenge Friends" as "Challenge your family" or "Beat your own best," and defer any true cross-household social to a post-F30 Pillar-5 project with VPC.

4.6 BE data model sketch (Firestore, aligned to existing conventions)

Ride the existing aggregate pipeline; add minimal collections (BE rule: add fields when the feature lands):

  • child-xp-events (append-only ledger, idempotent): { childId, localDate, source: enum, eventId (dedup, mirrors the aggregate marker pattern), xp, createdAt }. Written by the same message/game-completion triggers that already fold child-day-aggregates; eventId gates redelivery exactly like applyMessageCreate().
  • Extend child-day-aggregates (or a sibling child-xp-daily) with xpBySource: Record<source, number> + xpTotal — folded in the same idempotent transaction, so the daily rollup + parent dashboard get XP for free via the existing off-path narrative generator (findNarrativeDirty/markNarrativeGenerated).
  • child.gamification on the child profile (or a child-progress/{childId} doc): { level, xpTotal, xpToNext, badges: [{id, earnedAt}], stickers: [{setId, itemId, earnedAt}], consistencyDays }. Recomputable from the ledger (self-heal, like reconcileDay).
  • Family board: query the parent's children + parent as members; compute weekly/all-time XP sums server-side; return names+avatars only for same-account members (never cross-account).
  • Badge/level evaluation: a server-side evaluator runs on each XP fold (or nightly) comparing cumulative counters to thresholds; emits newlyEarned badges/levels in the callable response so the FE can fire the celebration. Keep thresholds in a config constant, not scattered.
  • Compliance: XP/badge data is child PI-adjacent → covered by the written retention policy; family board discloses only within the household (no third-party disclosure). No engagement push to the child.

4.7 FE surfaces (where XP toasts + celebrations appear)

  • Inline micro-celebration on every grant (Duolingo-ABC pattern, never a blocking modal): a small XP chip float + the existing haptic (feedback.success sized S/M/L). Wire the currently-unwired success()/playLandmarkPattern() here.
  • Home dashboard (child-dashboard): keep the existing Today's Goal + Accomplishments cards; add a level ring + XP-to-next (Apple-Rings-style, calm) and a sticker shelf entry point (the "collection-shelf moment" the code was built for).
  • Big celebration on level-up / set-completion: avatar (F26) high-five/cheer scene + playLandmarkPattern() → settle (arousal arc; no rapid flashing; ≤2–3 s peak).
  • Badges + Leaderboard screens: port the Figma frames — Badges grid as-is (minus friends badge), Leaderboard defaulting to Self/Family tabs, one consistent currency label (XP).
  • All juice content-relevant, not extra (Takacs), and every sound gated by the existing Sound preference; haptic-first.

4.8 Parent-dashboard visibility (required, not optional)

Parent visibility of the reward economy is a compliance expectation (REGULATORY §7 row 20). Surface in the existing parent dashboard (fold into the nightly daily-summary/dashboard.service):

  • This week's XP + level, badges earned, current collection progress, consistency days.
  • A "reward settings" panel with per-lever toggles, conservative defaults: sound on/off (already exists), family leaderboard on/off (off by default), any streak feature on/off (off by default), celebration intensity.
  • Framed positively ("what Mia explored," not surveillance), consistent with F32's report direction.

4.9 The single riskiest design decision

Whether to ship a visible, competitive multi-person leaderboard / streak at all. The Figma design and the "super / Challenge-Friends" ask pull toward exactly the competitive PBL mechanics that (a) have the weakest efficacy evidence in this age band (Kurnaz g=0.309), (b) risk discouraging the 8–9s who compare and the low-confidence kids inflated praise harms, and (c) are the highest-COPPA/DSA-scrutiny surface in the entire app (cross-household disclosure, friend-list collection, loss-framed streaks). The safe, still-"super"-feeling resolution — collection-first + self/family-scoped + no-loss consistency + cosmetic levels + character-delivered celebration — is also the one the codebase is already built to support and the one every premium reference app (Duolingo ABC, Khan Kids, Sago, Pok Pok, Pokémon Smile) actually ships. Recommendation: build that; make "leaderboard" mean self+family only; make "Challenge Friends" mean "challenge your family / beat your best"; defer any true cross-household social to a post-F30, VPC-gated project.
